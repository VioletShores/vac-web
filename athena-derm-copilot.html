JAMA Dermatology — Skin Cancer Surveillance for Transplant Patients
New
David S. Lee, MD · 18 min · Hosted by Adewole Adamson, MD
Read summary
Solid organ transplant recipients have significantly elevated skin cancer risk due to immunosuppression. Dr Lee describes a structured surveillance program that stratifies patients by risk level and schedules appropriate screening intervals. Key takeaway for your practice: any transplant patients in your care should be on an annual full-body check at minimum, with high-risk patients every 3-6 months. The program showed improved early detection rates and reduced advanced-stage diagnoses.
For your practice: Any transplant patients on your list should be flagged for structured surveillance — the evidence for risk-stratified screening is now strong.
🎙️
AAD Dialogues — GLP-1 Agonists and Skin Disease
New
Lindsey Bordone, MD · 20 min · CME eligible
Read summary
With GLP-1 agonists (Ozempic, Wegovy) now prescribed to millions, dermatologists are seeing unexpected skin effects. Dr Bordone covers the emerging evidence: potential improvement in hidradenitis suppurativa and psoriasis through anti-inflammatory mechanisms, but also reports of rapid facial aging ("Ozempic face") from dramatic weight loss. Practical guidance on counselling patients about skin changes during GLP-1 therapy and when to adjust treatment plans.
Relevant to your practice: With GLP-1 prescriptions rising rapidly, you may be seeing more patients presenting with these skin changes.

Dermasphere — 3D Total-Body Photography for Melanoma
Based on JAMA Derm RCT by Monika Janda, PhD
Read summary
A randomised clinical trial evaluated 3D total-body photography systems in high-risk melanoma patients. The technology captures the entire skin surface in standardised lighting, allowing AI-assisted comparison over time to detect new or changed lesions. Results showed improved detection of melanoma in situ compared to standard clinical photography. The Dermasphere hosts discuss practical considerations: cost, space requirements, patient acceptance, and whether the technology is ready for private practice.
For Sam: This connects directly to your photo triage concept — what you're building manually, these systems automate at full-body scale.

Nature: AI vs Skin Cancer — The Future of Dermatology Diagnosis
England's NHS trialling DERM platform by Skin Analytics
Read AI summary
The NHS is running a clinical trial of the DERM platform — patients photograph skin lesions with a smartphone using a dermoscopic lens attachment, images upload to an AI platform that flags urgent cases. The system matched physician-level accuracy in early trials. Key concern raised: limited data on effectiveness for darker skin tones, and some dermatologists worry about removing trained clinicians from the triage step. The technology is promising but not yet a replacement for clinical judgment.
Why this matters: Your photo triage feature follows the same architecture — patient-submitted images with AI flagging. You're already building what the NHS is trialling.

Memorial Sloan Kettering: AI for Melanoma Detection
Dr Veronica Rotemberg on AI models for skin imaging
Read AI summary
Dr Rotemberg explains how AI can analyse both dermoscopic and total body images to identify high-risk lesions patients might not notice. Melanoma caught early is curable with surgery alone — making AI-assisted early detection potentially lifesaving. She stresses that AI performance depends heavily on image quality and training data diversity, and calls for rigorous clinical trials before deployment.
Key insight: "Melanoma can be cured if caught early — AI detection at the photography stage is the biggest opportunity in dermatology right now."
